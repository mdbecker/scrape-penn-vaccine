COVID-19 Vaccines

Thursday, February 4, 2021—With the FDA's emergency authorization of the Pfizer-BioNTech and Moderna COVID-19 vaccines, Penn Medicine is working with our medical experts and the government to get the vaccine to health care staff and high-risk patients. 

We are giving out the vaccines in the most ethical, safest and quickest way possible. Our teams have made sure that everything needed for the vaccines is set up, including supplies and personal protective equipment (PPE), shipment, storage systems, and technology.

Our ultimate goal is to ensure that everyone who wants a vaccine gets it. 

Information about the vaccines is constantly changing, and we will update this page frequently as we learn more.

If you would like us to keep you informed about the availability of the COVID-19 vaccine at Penn Medicine, please complete our Vaccine Interest Form.

Giving Out the COVID-19 Vaccine to Patients

Vaccinations are happening based on place of residence (state and county)

The Departments of Health of Pennsylvania, New Jersey and the city of Philadelphia have released new guidance for giving out the vaccine, starting with those who are 65 and older (75 and older in Philadelphia), or who have certain pre-existing conditions. 

The city of Philadelphia just launched a vaccine interest form. We encourage you to register for appropriate county and/or state government sites for notifications and to explore all options for you and your family in each phase of the roll-out, given the supply limitations.  

Penn Medicine Radnor has submitted an application to the State of Pennsylvania to be designated as a vaccination location. Once we receive notification from the state we will communicate with eligible patients as to how they can schedule.

We are awaiting guidance for the other counties that surround Philadelphia and encourage you to monitor your county’s health department website for updates. If you are a patient at Penn Medicine Chester County Hospital, Penn Medicine Lancaster General Health or Penn Medicine Princeton Health, please check their website for more information. 

Penn Medicine is vaccinating patients on a limited basis, based on geography 

Penn Medicine has started vaccinating patients who are Philadelphia residents and have one of the following high-risk conditions:

  • 75 years of age or older
  • Cancer
  • Chronic kidney disease
  • Organ transplant
  • Diabetes
  • Sickle Cell Anemia

Due to a very limited supply, we are contacting patients who are at the highest risk first. As our supply of vaccine increases, we will continue to contact patients who meet the eligibility requirements to schedule their vaccine. Given the current rate of vaccine allocation, the process is expected to take weeks for this phase. At this time, we are not able to schedule COVID-19 vaccine appointments for patients who call us.

In addition to hospital staff and people with high-risk medical conditions, people who live in nursing homes and staff who work in nursing homes are getting the vaccine early. After that, the vaccine will be given out to other essential workers, and then the general population.

Types of COVID-19 Vaccines

Two vaccines, developed by pharmaceutical companies Pfizer-BioNTech and Moderna, are available to frontline health care workers and high-risk patients. Both vaccines are showing about 95% efficacy in their preliminary reviews.

The US Food & Drug Administration (FDA) authorized the Pfizer and BioNTech vaccine on December 11 and the Moderna vaccine on December 18.

Have questions about the COVID-19 vaccines?

Check out our frequently asked questions (FAQs) for more information on vaccine safety, who should get the vaccine, and when it will be available.

mRNA Vaccine Technology Developed at Penn Medicine

The Pfizer-BioNTech and Moderna vaccines use the mRNA technology developed at Penn by infectious disease expert Drew Weissman, MD, PhD, along with longtime research collaborator Katalin Karikó, PhD, an adjunct associate professor. Dr. Weissman has been studying mRNA vaccines for decades. This technology could change the way future vaccines are made to prevent countless other diseases.
